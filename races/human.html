Human

This page contains all the standard adventuring rules collected in one place for convenience.

Human Adventurers: Exploration and Adventuring Rules

1. Humans are the default adventurer race

When creating a character, roll attributes, choose a character class, and choose a race only if the character is non-human. A human character therefore has no separate racial package to select: the character is primarily defined by class, attributes, equipment, alignment, and play decisions.

(Chapter 1: Getting Started — Character Creation)

2. Non-human adventurers exist only if the Referee allows them

The Referee decides which non-human races, if any, are available in the campaign. This means human adventurers are always the baseline assumption, while Dwarves, Elves, Halflings, or other races depend on the campaign.

(Chapter 3: Character Races)

3. Humans may use the normal character classes without racial limits

The listed character classes are Cleric, Fighter, Magic-user, and optional Thief. Humans do not have a racial rule limiting them to particular classes or maximum levels. By contrast, Dwarves, Elves, and Halflings have race-specific class choices and typical level limits.

(Chapter 2: Character Classes; Chapter 3: Character Races)

4. Dwarves have adventuring limits and abilities humans do not

A Dwarf may usually be a Fighter or Thief, if Thieves are used, and is typically limited to 6th level. Dwarves have no weapon or armor restrictions, just like human Fighters. They take only half normal damage from giants, ogres, trolls, and similar giant-type creatures. Underground, Dwarves are especially good at noticing traps, sloping passages, and construction: 1–4 on a d6 when searching, or 1–2 on a d6 when merely passing by. Dwarves also receive +4 on saving throws against magic, whether or not the alternate Saving Throw Matrix is used.

(Chapter 3: Character Races — The Dwarf)

5. Elves have adventuring limits and abilities humans do not

An Elf may use either the Fighter or Magic-user advancement chart, chosen at the start of an adventure, and is typically limited to 4th level as a Fighter and 8th level as a Magic-user. When adventuring as a Magic-user, an Elf uses the same weapon and armor limits as a Magic-user, except that magic armor may still be worn. Elves gain +1 to hit or damage against goblins, orcs, intelligent undead, and lycanthropes, and they are immune to paralysis caused by undead such as ghouls. Elves are especially good at finding hidden and concealed doors: 1–4 on a d6 when searching, or 1–2 on a d6 when merely passing by.

(Chapter 3: Character Races — The Elf)

6. Halflings have adventuring limits and abilities humans do not

A Halfling may usually be a Fighter or Thief, if Thieves are used, and is typically limited to 4th level as a Fighter and 6th level as a Thief. Halflings have no weapon or armor restrictions, just like human Fighters. Giants, ogres, trolls, and similar giant-type creatures only inflict half normal damage against Halflings. Halflings receive +2 to hit when using missile weapons. When not in combat, they can be extremely stealthy; the Referee may decide success, or use a roll of 1–5 on a d6. Halflings also receive +4 on saving throws against magic, even if the Saving Throw Matrix is used.

(Chapter 3: Character Races — The Halfling)

7. Human movement is faster than Dwarf or Halfling movement at light and moderate loads

Base movement is measured in tens of feet per turn, with two moves allowed per turn. Humans use the Elf/Human column: carrying 0–75 lb. gives Move 12; 76–100 lb. gives Move 9; 101–150 lb. gives Move 6; and 151–300 lb. gives Move 3. Dwarves and Halflings use the slower column: Move 9, 6, 3, and 3 at the same load bands. Careful movement is half rate, normal movement is full rate, running is double rate, and combat movement is one-third rate.

(Chapter 5: Playing the Game — Movement)

8. Human overland movement is faster than Dwarf or Halfling overland movement

For wilderness travel by land, a Human or Elf has Move 12 and travels 2 hexes per day. A Dwarf or Halfling has Move 9 and travels 3/2 hexes per day.

(Chapter 7: Running the Game — Wilderness Movement Rates by Transport)

9. Humans open dungeon doors more easily than Dwarves and Halflings

Dungeon doors are heavy, and even unlocked ones are hard to open. Humans and Elves open an unlocked dungeon door on a roll of 1–2 on a d6. Dwarves and Halflings have only a 1 in 6 chance. At the Referee’s option, Strength bonuses or penalties apply to this roll.

(Chapter 5: Playing the Game — Dungeon Doors)

10. Humans hear less well at dungeon doors than non-humans

When listening at doors, Humans hear noise on a roll of 1 on a d6. Non-humans hear noise on a roll of 1–2 on a d6. Success means the character hears something, but may not know what caused the sound.

(Chapter 5: Playing the Game — Listening at Doors)

11. Humans use the normal secret-door search rule

Any player character who actively searches for a secret door finds one on a roll of 1–2 on a d6. Elves are better at this: they find secret doors on 1–4 on a d6 when searching, and may sense something wrong on 1–2 on a d6 merely by passing by. Searching takes one turn for each 10-foot by 10-foot area searched.

(Chapter 5: Playing the Game — Secret Doors)

12. Humans use the normal trap rules unless class abilities apply

Most traps and pits are triggered on a roll of 1–2 on a d6 when any player passes over the trigger. Dwarves have a special advantage with stonework traps: they detect such traps on 1–4 on a d6 when actively searching, or 1–2 on a d6 when merely passing by. A character who falls into a pit trap takes 1d6 damage per 10 feet fallen.

(Chapter 5: Playing the Game — Traps)

13. Human hirelings are the standard price baseline

When hiring assistants for adventuring work, the listed prices are for one week of service and apply to humans only. Demi-humans cost more to hire, and may require more than gold.

(Chapter 4: Equipment — Hiring Assistants)

14. Human Armor Class starts from the normal unarmored baseline

In descending Armor Class, an unarmored human has AC 9. Armor lowers AC, and lower AC is better. In ascending Armor Class, an unarmored person has AAC [10]. Armor adds to AAC, and higher AAC is better.

(Chapter 4: Equipment — Calculating Armor Class)
